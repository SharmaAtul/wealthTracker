Asset Allocation

 

What is asset allocation?

An investment strategy that aims to balance risk and reward by apportioning a portfolio's assets according to an individual's goals, risk tolerance and investment horizon.

Securities and assets are generally divided into the following main asset classes:

  • Cash.
  • Fixed Interest.
  • Property.
  • Australian Shares.
  • International Shares

Each asset class has its own characteristics in relation to risk, volatility, income and return.

Who will benefit from proper asset allocation?

There is no simple formula that can find the right asset allocation for every individual. However, the consensus among most financial professionals is that asset allocation is one of the most important decisions that investors make. In other words, your selection of individual securities is secondary to the way you allocate your investment in stocks, bonds, and cash and equivalents, which will be the principal determinants of your investment results.

When should you review your asset allocation?

Your asset allocation requires reviewing if your circumstances in your life change. That's why it's a good idea to re-examine your life insurance needs at least every few years and certainly when big changes, or life events, occur.

Some life events that affecting your asset allocation needs are:

  • Having a child.
  • Getting married.
  • Buying a home.
  • Changing jobs or getting a pay rise.
  • Taking on debt.
  • Supporting aging parents.
  • Changes in your business.
  • Changes in marital status.
  • Planning for college.
  • Planning for retirement.

Besides the above mentioned life changing events there are also other reasons that requires reviewing of your asset allocation including:

  • Increase or decrease of value of your existing assets
  • A change in legislation
  • A change in risk attitudes
  • A change of the characteristics of your existing investments.
  • Global trends including economic uncertainty
  • Your cash requirements including any future expenses

What is cash and equivalents?

Cash investments generally provide clients with instant or almost instant access and return interest as income which is fully taxable. Long term return expectations of cash are about 2% to 2.5% above inflation and the volatility is very low. Typical cash instruments are:

  • Direct savings accounts.
  • Cash management accounts.
  • Internet savings accounts.
  • Term deposits.

Who needs exposure to cash and equivalents?

Clients that wish to have instant access to their funds to cover short term expenses and wish to have no fluctuation in their investment exposure.

As cash is only returning on average 2% above inflation, exposure to this asset class is barely helping with increasing wealth. However everyone has short term expenses which generally require instant access or short term access. .

Fixed Interest

Fixed interest investments are mostly government bonds, corporate bonds, mortgages and hybrid securities which generally operate in the same way as loans. The income return is usually in the form of regular interest payments for an agreed period of time.

Generally fixed interest return is on average a little higher than cash with long term average returns of 3% above inflation. It is possible to gain exposure to Australian and or International Fixed Interest

Who needs exposure to fixed interest?

Clients that wish to have minimal movement to their investment value and wish to park their funds for a minimum time frame of between 1 and 3 years.

Property

Property investments can be direct investments or indirect investment by purchasing property securities. The most common form of property investment is a clients home of residence, which can be sold tax free. Investment property can take many forms, including retail, commercial, hotel, industrial or infrastructure.

To gain exposure to direct property an investor requires a large amount of capital. This can be avoided by purchasing indirect property securities which are shares in property investments that are listed on share markets.

Who needs exposure to property?

Property is a growth asset giving investors access to income in the form of rent and growth in the form of increase of value.

As property values go up and down, it is possible to be exposed to a loss and for this reason property investments require generally a minimum investment time frame of 5 years.

Finally, property average long term return is about 4% above inflation but has been higher between 2000 and 2009.

International shares generally work the same as Australian shares. The additional benefit is the increased opportunity to invest in a much wider range of countries (eg Europe, Asia) and a broader selection of companies (eg Microsoft, Johnson and Johnson) outside Australia. However currency valuations can negatively affect performance.

Why do you need exposure to International Shares?

Different countries move in different economic cycles. Other countries are structurally changing and are on their way from a developed world to a first world economy. To gain exposure to the potential capital appreciation of companies active in those countries it is important to consider some international exposure

Australia share market only represent 2.5% of the world total capitalisation. Considering the increased globalisation and to access industries not available in Australia, International Share exposure should be a part of an investor growth exposure.
